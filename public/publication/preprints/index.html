Good Me Bad Me: Does Valence Influence Self-Prioritization During Perceptual Decision-Making?

Chuan-Peng Hu, Yuxuan Lan, Macrae, C. Neil, Jie Sui
April 2019
PDF Code Dataset Project Slides DOI Custom Link
Image credit: Unsplash

Abstract

People display systematic priorities to self-related stimuli and information with a positive (vs. negative) valence. The extent to which these basic stimulus properties interact during decision-making, however, remains unspecified. To explore this issue, here we combined self-relevance and valence using a standard shape-label association task in which participants first learned the associations (e.g., circle/good-other, triangle/good-self, diamond/bad-other, square/good-self), and then completed shape-label matching and shape-categorization tasks whereby attention was directed to different aspects of the stimuli (i.e., self-relevance or valence-relevance). The results revealed that responses were more efficient to the good-self shape than to other shapes, regardless of the task that was undertaken. A hierarchical drift diffusion model (HDDM) analysis indicated that this good-self prioritization effect was underpinned by differences in the rate of information uptake. These findings demonstrate that the presence of good-self stimuli automatically activates a core identity-based representation (‘good me’) that facilitates decision-making.
